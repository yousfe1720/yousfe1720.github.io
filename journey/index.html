ORIGIN / SELF-DIRECTED FOUNDATIONS

FROM SELF-DIRECTED LEARNING TO AUTONOMOUS SYSTEMS

My engineering journey did not begin in a laboratory or inside a well-equipped research program. It began with curiosity, internet access, and regular visits to my city library, where I searched for books about physics, aerodynamics, aircraft, and the principles governing how machines move and operate.

At that stage, I did not have a clear roadmap or consistent technical guidance. I learned by reading, experimenting, making mistakes, and returning to the problem with a better question.

REJECTION INTO ENGINEERING DISCIPLINE

Learning Through Rejection

My early ideas were not immediately taken seriously. My age created skepticism, and some of my first project concepts were not mature enough to demonstrate their full potential.

Rather than treating that rejection as a reason to stop, I began treating it as engineering feedback. I became more disciplined about documentation, validation, system architecture, testing, and the evidence required to defend a technical idea professionally.

ENGINEERING FAILURE CASE / S/S1

FAILURE BECAME THE REDESIGN BRIEF.

Between 2022 and 2023, I developed and tested S/S1 as a resilient avionics and counter-UAS system integrated with the Sky Shark platform. One of the most important moments in the project was not a successful test; it was a destructive failure.

An early version failed because the hardware and power architecture did not adequately manage the difference in voltage and energy requirements between connected components. The original signal architecture was also concentrated in a single direction, restricting intended angular coverage.

I redesigned the hardware and power architecture, revised protection and distribution between subsystems, improved separation between flight-critical electronics and the mission system, and expanded the intended angular coverage to approximately 240 degrees.

COMMERCIAL MATURITY

FROM PROTOTYPES TO A PILOT-READY SYSTEM.

By March 2026, I completed the final pilot-ready version of SKOLDERN, an industrial predictive-maintenance platform designed to help factories identify developing equipment faults before they cause costly unplanned failures.

The system was validated using NASA C-MAPSS turbofan datasets, while its Rapid Inspection Engine demonstrated the ability to process 20,000 sensor readings in under 60 seconds. SKOLDERN is now ready for industrial pilot testing and is being introduced to potential commercial customers.

MIR-1

CAD and Simulation Complete

I also completed the CAD architecture and simulation program for MIR-1, a tracked robotic inspection system connected to SKOLDERN. MIR-1 is designed to collect visual, thermal, acoustic, vibration, and three-dimensional mapping data from hazardous industrial areas.

Physical prototyping remains the next development stage.
